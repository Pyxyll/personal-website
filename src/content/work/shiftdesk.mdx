## The problem

Publishing a rota is the easy half. The hard half is what happens after it
changes — someone calls in sick, someone drops a late surgery, and the cover has
to be found and validated in the hour you have to find it.

Most rota tools treat that as a broadcast problem: notify everyone, let a human
sort it out. That puts the manager back on the phone, and it means a gap is only
as visible as whoever last looked at the sheet.

ShiftDesk treats it as a constraint problem. Open shifts are a first-class state
that anyone eligible can claim without asking, and the system refuses the
combinations that create the next mess — so cover gets found by the people who
have the availability, not by the person with the phone.

## What it does

The same week of data is shown through three lenses.

**Staff** see the rota as themselves. Open shifts are dashed and amber; the ones
they hold are outlined in teal. They can claim an open shift, drop one they no
longer want, and mark a day unavailable. You can switch which employee you're
signed in as from the header — the rota, the hour count and the notifications all
follow.

**Managers** see coverage rather than personal schedule: a fill bar per day, a
count of the gaps, who has marked themselves off, and hours-against-target for
the whole team. They can assign an open shift, unassign someone, or add a new
open shift to any day.

**Stats** rolls the same live state up into filled-vs-open hours by role,
coverage per week, and hours per person. It is computed from state, not seeded —
claim a few shifts and the bars move.

## The rules that make it safe

A scheduling tool is only useful if it refuses the bad rota. Three rules do most
of that work here:

- **No double-booking.** Claiming a shift that overlaps one you already hold is
  rejected, and the toast names the shift it clashes with rather than just saying
  "no".
- **Availability is respected.** Marking a day off blocks you from claiming on it,
  and hides you from the manager's assign picker for that day.
- **The picker only offers people who can actually work it.** It filters to the
  matching role, drops anyone with an overlapping shift, and drops anyone who is
  off — so an assignment is valid by construction rather than by the manager
  remembering.

Marking a day off deliberately does *not* strip shifts you already hold. It warns
you that you still hold them and leaves the decision with you, because silently
dropping someone's shift is a worse failure than an inconsistent-looking day.

Every change writes to a per-shift history and a session activity feed, so
"who dropped Friday?" has an answer.

## Scope, and what a second site changes

The demo is one week at one site, with six people, so the rules are legible in
about thirty seconds of clicking. That's a demo decision, not a ceiling.

The part that carries over is the eligibility model: a shift is claimable by you
if the role matches, you're free, and it doesn't overlap something you already
hold. Adding sites mostly means adding one more term to that check — which sites
you're qualified and willing to travel to — and letting a shift belong to a
location. Claiming, clash rejection, the assign picker and the coverage rollups
all keep working off the same predicate.

What genuinely gets harder with a group is not the rota, it's the questions
across it: who can cover Ealing on Thursday when the bank staff are already at
Croydon, and which sites are quietly carrying the overtime. That's a reporting
and float-pool problem, and it's where I'd want to talk about your actual
constraints before designing anything.

## Notes on the build

The entire scenario — rota, availability, notifications, per-shift history — is
one reducer. That's what keeps an action like *claim* honest: a rule check, then
a state patch, with no route through the code where the toast congratulates you
on a shift the grid didn't actually give you.

Motion here is load-bearing rather than decorative. Claiming a shift or changing
a filter reorders the columns, so cards animate from where they were to where
they land — positions measured before the state change, replayed after — and you
can keep your eye on the shift you were looking at instead of hunting for it
again. Open shifts pulse their dashed border, which turns an under-covered week
into something you notice rather than something you count. All of it collapses
under `prefers-reduced-motion`.

The page itself is static HTML; only the demo hydrates. Its styles are scoped so
that a light, rounded product UI and this site's dark brutalist theme can share a
page without bleeding into one another — the demo is a product, and it should
look like one rather than like a page of my website.
