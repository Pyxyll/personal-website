## What it is

A customer asks where a book is. The honest answer is usually a pointed finger
and "Fiction, somewhere on the left" — which is fine until the shop is busy, or
the person asking is holding a list.

Pagerr answers the question spatially. You type a title or an author, and the
shelf it lives on lights up on a floor plan of the store, with a dotted route
drawn from the checkout to the shelf. Add a few books and it stops being a lookup
and becomes a trip: the stops get ordered into a sensible walk, numbered on the
map, and the route redraws to take them all in.

This is a rebuild of client work, with a seeded catalogue standing in for the real
one. The version that shipped also talked to barcode scanners and store hardware,
which is not something a portfolio page can honestly show you.

## The part that's actually hard

Drawing a line from A to B is trivial and useless: it goes straight through the
bookcases. A route is only worth showing if it's a route a person could walk.

So the floor plan isn't decoration, it's the data structure. Shelves have real
positions, and the gaps between them are modelled as corridors — four running
across the shop, four running up it. Every shelf knows which corridor point you'd
stand at to reach it. A leg of a route is then a small problem: get from one
corridor position to another, using whichever crossing corridor costs least, and
turn the corners.

That constraint is what makes the rest fall out. Once a route has a real length,
ordering a multi-book trip is just repeatedly walking to the nearest unvisited
stop. It's a greedy nearest-neighbour tour, not an optimal one — for a dozen
stops in a grid of aisles it gives you the order you'd have picked anyway, and it
runs instantly, which matters more than the last few metres of savings.

## Peeking inside a shelf

Knowing the shelf gets you within about a metre. The last metre is the annoying
one, so clicking any shelf opens it: a bookcase of spines, with the catalogued
titles darker than the filler and the one you searched for highlighted and
pulsing, captioned with the row it's on.

The filler spines are generated, not authored — a shelf holding six real books
would look absurd, and hand-writing five hundred fake ones is a bad use of a
life. They come from a PRNG seeded by the shelf's own id, so the arrangement is
stable: the same shelf looks the same every time you open it, rather than
reshuffling itself under you on each render.

## Notes on the build

The routing lives in its own module of pure functions over coordinates, which is
the main reason the interesting logic is legible — and checkable — at all.
Geometry tangled into a component is geometry nobody will touch again. Keeping it
separable also let me assert the only thing that really matters: sample a few
hundred points along the rendered path and confirm that none of them land inside
a bookcase.

The route animation is two effects layered. The dashes march along the path
forever, which reads as direction of travel, while a mask wipes along the same
path to reveal it the first time. The reveal has to be a mask rather than a dash
offset, because you can't animate one `stroke-dashoffset` in two directions for
two different reasons.

The shop's warm paper and terracotta don't leak into this site's dark theme, and
the dark theme doesn't leak into the shop — the demo's styles are scoped, so the
two can sit on one page and each keep its own world.
